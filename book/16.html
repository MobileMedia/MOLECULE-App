4.4 Context Blogger

The mobile media group works on approaches on how to support learning in context. We have developed several systems involving sensor technology, mobile end user devices, and instructional designs for blended learning scenarios. In the Learning Media Lab we have an infrastructure to prototype and showcase new solutions easily and build prototypes.

Language is a typical example of something that is widely used across contexts. Language learning takes place in different settings, for example, in a structured setting in an official language course in a educational institution, or a more unstructured, and common day-to-day setting in which language is acquired in a random manner. Additionally, the type of language learnt depends on the situation; some require informal daily speech, while other settings, i.e. business negotiations, require more formal language. Furthermore, language learning is addressed towards a certain community, most often a community of native or near-native practitioners, which uses a community-specific jargon (Petersen & Divitini, 2005). Especially, in an increasingly international world, acquiring this community-specific language becomes more and more important. Particularly, non-native speakers have a demand for just-in-time, situation-specific vocabulary to communicate in a more effective and efficient way. In our prototypes for mobile contrextualised language learning we focused on the following functionalities:

  • Acquiring language on the move, tailored to specific situations,
  • Active use of the language, either by communication with native peers,
  • Creation of learning content, either contextualised or de-contextualised,
  • Commenting on peers,
  • Discussion with peers,
  • Memorisation and repetition,
  • Planning, structuring, and self-monitoring of learning,
  • Learning by interaction in the real-world.

In addition, countless situations could be defined that are used to contextualise the available language content, for instance standard situations as introducing yourself, ordering at a restaurant, bargaining in a shop, etc. In several empirical evaluation and lab studies we compared the effects of contextual filtering, task structures, automatic history recording, and creation of personal annotation on learning outcomes as vocabulary learning.

In one experiment individuals received tasks as to prepare an indian meal for a visiting friend and they had to physycally walk around in the lab and collect the words and ingridients for preparing the meal by typing in the numbers of the ingredient.

Main outcomes have been that task structures seem to be a strong contextual factor having a signitificant impact on learning structures. Furthermore experiments have shown that contextual filtering should still give users freedom to explore and not filter the vocabulary recommendation down to single words but filtering for rooms or actual semantic contexts seems to work best.
