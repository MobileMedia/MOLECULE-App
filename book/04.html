2.2 Feedback

Ask yourself:

When do you need feedback in a learning process and how could mobile devices help here?

Feedback is a central concept in learning. A huge number of studies and experiments have been done in the past about the effects of feedback in education and learning. A good overview is given in Mory (2004). According to Mory, feedback is a special type of response on an action. In learning feedback serves as a way for learners to evaluate the success of their actions for reaching their learning goals. This evaluation is also called reflection and is essential for self-regulated and self-directed learning. 

Three functional types of feedback can get distinguished in education. 

  1. Reinforcement feedback
  2. Validation feedback 
  3. Motivational feedback

Reinforcement feedback is similar to validation feedback with regard to its relation to the learning content, but it emphasizes reinforcing correct answers. This type of feedback has its origins in programmed instruction. However, this idea has been abandoned by research, because for the solitary focus on positive reinforcement there was not significant effect on the learning process found. 

Today, validation feedback is most commonly associated when feedback is mentioned in TEL contexts. This type of feedback directly refers to the validity of the results of an action in relation to predefined goals. This type of feedback is frequently related to responses on the results of assessments. A variation of validation feedback is corrective feedback. This feedback highlights mistakes and provides guidance for avoiding these errors in future activities. Validation feedback requires good knowledge about the learning goals and resources (input), the learning activities (loop), and the subsequential and final learning outcomes (output). 

Motivational feedback focuses on supporting learners in their engagement in the learning process. This type of feedback gets increasingly relevant because the different instructional approaches including validation feedback appear not always to have positive effect for different learner types. Engaging learners in learning depends on their primarily task disposition as well as on their personal contexts. The task disposition describes how a learner interprets a task's process and its outcomes for future actions. Purely validation oriented feedback may lead learners into learning inefficient strategies or coping strategies (such as learning helplessness).

A variation of motivation feedback is incentive feedback. 

In the Mobile Media group we are interested in the effects of feedback loops in mobile and ubiquitous learning settings, i.e. what is the effect of giving notification on certain times of the day, how can we use sensor data for realtime feedback in learning, what changes when feedback is embedded in our environment with ambient displays.
