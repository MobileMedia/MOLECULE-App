4.4 Ubiquitous Moodle

Over the past years several attempts for connecting Moodle to mobile devices have been made. While past attempts are focused on making the functions of the virtual learning environment (VLE) available on mobile devices, our concept of ubiquitous moodle blends relevant information form Moodle into spatial learning environments that are equipped with ubiquitous computing devices.

Making a virtual learning environment available on a mobile device follows the same single device interaction approach as the one that is common in desktop computing environments. From this viewpoint the main challenge for enabling the mobile use of a VLE is the adaptation to a special display type, while most other aspects of the underpinning educational paradigm remain fixed. Features of personalizing learning experiences based on the learners’ mobility and their changing information needs in different contexts is typically not considered by these developments.

However, the mobile revolution in computing systems goes beyond personal computing and the fixation to a single device. Information systems grow increasingly complex and divers into our daily lifes. It is not uncommon anymore that people are active in environments with multiple information systems and interfaces. Therefore, we could say that many of us have actually left the personalised computing world and now live in device ecologies.

Compared to traditional web-based learning environments, learning in device ecologies has distinct challenges.

  •  The foremost challenge is that a learner or mentor may interact with a different system than the one that should provide access to the learning resources.
  • The second challenge is to arrange interactions and information presentation in ways that do not overload a person with information queues.
  • The third challenge is to identify the features of a spatial learning environment. The same learning activities might get performed differently depending on the technical setting that surrounds a learner.

In the typical web-based request-response scenario these challenges do not exist and are therefore ignored in the design of most VLEs. In these scenarios the access to information is always directly related to a person's interactions. The number of information queues is limited by the capabilities of the device's interface. Finally, it is considered that all learning activities are performed in similar settings.

The Ubiquitous Moodle prototype tackled these challenges by integrating the Actuator-Indicator Architecture into a Moodle system. This allows to extend normal Moodle Courses to utilize the features of devices ecologies in labs, lecture halls or at home.
