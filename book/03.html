2.1 Reflection

Ask yourself:
Which role does reflection play in your life and your learning experiences? When do you reflect? What is the result of such a reflection process?

Reflection before, during and after action is assumed to be an essential factor of learning. Its practice at school is supposed to gradually develop learners' awareness of what helps and hampers a consistent orchestration of the various dimensions of their learning processes (Ertmer & Newby, 1996). Prominent authors endorse the importance for learners to develop observations about their own learning experiences, see for instance Schön’s (1994, p. 114)ladder of reflection, (1994, p. 114), Bateson’s (Bateson, 1977, p. 314) notion of deutero-learning (Bateson, 1977, p. 314) or and Kolb’s (1984)"reflective observer" stage. Integrating reflective thinking skills into curriculum and classroom practice has recently gained momentum (Heargraves, 2005). It is generally claimed that stimulating reflective skills will empower knowledge workers to cope with requests for new knowledge acquisition and ongoing personal development in a variety of contexts (Peters, 2004, p. 184).

Reflection is akin to constructs like:

  • Metacognitive development (Gama, 2004a, p. 24): The considerable amount of research conducted in this field over more than 30 years demonstrates that instructional practice should not simply aim at engaging learners at the level of presenting information for understanding and use, but also direct them at meta-levels of learning (Flavell, 1979);
  • Learning to learn (Watkins, 2001, p. 4): The European Commission heralded it as one of the 8 key competencies for lifelong learning (2006) and a key factor for educational endeavours;
  • Self-regulated learning (Isaacson & Fujita, 2006; Ridley, Schutz, Glanz, & Weinstein, 1992; Zimmerman, 1995). Research in this domain constantly stresses the relationship between reflection, self-monitoring and learning.

The proximity of reflection to these notions has lead to a variety of different interpretations and understandings of the word reflection amongst teachers and educational researchers. As for this article, reflection is defined as an active process of witnessing one’s own learning experience and evaluating it from different perspectives. Reflection is regarded a means by which learners can build and evolve a mental model of the learning tasks they are committed to and of their position inside those tasks (Seel, Al-Diban, & Blumschein, 2002), so that appropriate directions of actions can be procured. The co-ordinating idea of this paper is that reflection can be prompted by using "reflection amplifiers", that is, structured opportunities for students to examine and evaluate their learning experience (Joy Amulya, 2004).

Despite the claimed importance of reflection for learning, Watkins (2001) or Claxton (2006) observe that there is a lack of clear theoretical approaches and tested practices.
